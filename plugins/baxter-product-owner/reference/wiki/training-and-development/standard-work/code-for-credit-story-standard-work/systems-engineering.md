# Training and Development/Standard Work/Code-for-Credit Story Standard Work/Systems Engineering

System Engineers operate as Liaison between Product Management and R&D. They translate voice of customer to product requirements. They develop, allocate, and manage stakeholder needs and system level requirements where a System Requirement identifies a capability or condition that a system must have or meet to satisfy a need or a standard.

Challenge: Traditional waterfall beings with heavy documentation prior to development for a predictive approach to achieve stability through exhaustive up-front design definition. A different approach is desired under the Agile construct to iterate by failing and learning fast. Systems engineering artifacts should be defined dynamically within the scope of the planned iteration. 

Responsibilities of a System Engineer include:
- [ ] Gather and document stakeholder needs
- [ ] Document use cases and user workflows
- [ ] Translate needs to system requirements
- [ ] Represent Risk Controls and Regulations in System Requirements
- [ ] Define Technical Performance Measures
- [ ] Provide input for user story acceptance criteria to ensure outcomes are aligned with stakeholder needs.

A System Engineer should self-evaluate the System Requirements for the following criteria prior to peer review:

- Necessity: Every requirement must represent an essential characteristic of the system. If removing it would not degrade performance, safety, or compliance, it should not exist. Necessity ensures focus on critical elements rather than speculative “nice to haves.” 
- Implementation Independence: Requirements must describe what the system shall do, not how it will do it. This avoids premature design decisions. Implementation details belong in design artifacts, not in requirements documents. 
- Feasibility: Each requirement must be technically, financially, and temporally achievable. Unrealistic or unbounded statements create downstream instability. Feasibility analysis involves early trade studies, risk assessments, and cost performance modeling. 
- Verifiability: Requirements must be testable through one or more verification methods: inspection, analysis, demonstration, or test. Vague or subjective statements (“user-friendly,” “easy to maintain”) violate this principle unless supported by quantifiable criteria. 
- Unambiguity: Ambiguity is the enemy of execution. Requirements must have one and only one interpretation. Controlled vocabulary, consistent units of measure, and standardized structure minimize misinterpretation.
- Completeness: A complete requirement contains all information necessary to understand its intent and constraints. Incomplete requirements invite assumptions and inconsistent implementation. 
- Consistency: Requirements must not conflict internally or with external baselines, standards, or contractual documents. Enforce consistency through peer review and traceability. 
- Singularity: Each requirement expresses one concept only. Compound statements should be avoided.
- Traceability: Traceability links requirements upward to needs and downward to design, implementation, and test evidence. This bidirectionality allows auditors and engineers to follow cause and effect across the entire system. 
- Correctness: A requirement must accurately capture stakeholder intent. Incorrect requirements such as those derived from misinterpreted needs or obsolete standards, are worse than no requirement at all.

**Do I have to write System Requirements or User Needs for every user story?**
No. Multiple User Stories can be linked to singular Needs or Singular System Requirements.
