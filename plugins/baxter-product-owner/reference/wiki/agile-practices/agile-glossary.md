# Agile Practices/Agile Glossary

# Agile Glossary
See [SAFe Glossary - Scaled Agile Framework](https://framework.scaledagile.com/glossary)

1. **Acceptance Criteria**: The set of conditions that a User Story, Feature, or Capability must meet to be considered complete and acceptable by the stakeholders. They provide clear guidelines for the development team on what needs to be implemented and serve as a basis for testing the functionality.

1.  **Agile**: An iterative and incremental project management approach that emphasizes collaboration, adaptability, and continuous improvement.

1. **Agile Release Train (ART)**: a long-lived team of Agile teams that incrementally develops, delivers, and often operates one or more solutions within a development value stream.

1. **Architectural Runway**: Existing code, components, and technical infrastructure needed to implement near-term features with minimal redesign and delay. Intended to provide just enough foundation to enable flexibility and extensibility while ensuring teams can keep delivering value with efficiency.
   
1.  **(Program) Backlog**: A prioritized list of epics and features that product management and product owners will create, prioritize, and socialize during the project.

1.  **(Team) Backlog**: A prioritized list of user stories, tasks, and requirements that the team will work on during the project.

1. **Backlog Grooming**: The process of maintaining and refining the product backlog in an Agile project. This process involves reviewing, prioritizing, and estimating the user stories and tasks in the backlog to ensure that they are up-to-date, relevant, and ready for the team to work on. Backlog grooming can be done during sprint planning, sprint reviews, or dedicated grooming sessions, and it is an essential component of the Agile process.
    
1.  **Burn-down chart**: A graphical representation of the team's progress during a sprint, showing the remaining work versus time.
   
1.  **Daily Stand-up**: A brief, daily meeting where team members share their progress, discuss any obstacles, and coordinate their activities for the day.

1. **Definition of Done**: A clear set of criteria that define task completion eliminating ambiguity.  The criteria are agreed upon by the relevant team members.  It is not sufficient that the criteria are understand only by the implementor.  

1. **Effort**: The amount of time, resources, and energy required to complete a user story or task. Effort estimation is the process of determining the relative size or complexity of a user story or task, which helps the team plan and prioritize their work during a sprint.

1. **Epic**: An Epic is a significant solution development initiative that delivers business value or supports the Architectural Runway and future business functionality. Epics are typically large in scope and may span multiple Value Streams and Planning Intervals (PIs).
    
1.  **Estimation**: The process of determining the effort required to complete a user story or task.

1. **Feature**: A Feature is a piece of solution functionality that delivers business value and fulfills stakeholder need. Features may deliver customer-centric value or support business requirements such as architecture or infrastructure.

1. **Future State Team** A team that is envisioned to operate in an optimized manner, delivering value more effectively and efficiently in alignment with the organization's strategic goals. This concept is often discussed during workshops and planning sessions aimed at improving team performance and value delivery.
    
1.  **Iteration**: The SAFe Agile term for a sprint. This is a time-boxed period during which a cross-functional team collaborates to deliver a specific set of features or functionality.

1. **PI Goal**: A business stakeholder level desired goal for a PI. Typically these are tied to the product vision and agreed upon by Product Managers, Portfolio Leadership, and Business Owners. 

1. **PI Objective**: An objective created by a scrum team or ART during PI planning. This is a distillation of PI Goals down into Features/User Stories and then rewritten back into a business level, SMART objective. PI objectives are assigned business values during PI Planning and then reviewed during the I&A Workshop. See [SAFe Agile Ceremonies](https://dev.azure.com/FLC-NPD/FutureState/_wiki/wikis/Platform.wiki/74/Agile-Ceremonies-SAFe) for more details. 

1. **Planning Increment (PI)**: A timebox in which an Agile Release Train (ART) delivers incremental value. We define this to be a quarter (12 weeks) and divided into 6 sprints. A typical PI includes five development Iterations followed by one Innovation and Planning (IP) Iteration, allowing teams to reflect, innovate, and plan for the next PI.
    
1.  **Product Increment**: The sum of all completed work (features, user stories, and tasks) delivered during a specific time period, usually within an Iteration or a Program Increment.
   
1. **Product Owner (PO)**: The individual responsible for representing the needs and priorities of the customer or stakeholders. The PO is the primary point of contact for the team, and they are responsible for defining the product vision, prioritizing the backlog, and ensuring that the team delivers value to the customer. The PO works closely with the team to clarify requirements, answer questions, and provide feedback on the progress of the project. The PO is also responsible for managing the product backlog, ensuring that it is up-to-date and prioritized based on business value and customer needs. The PO plays a critical role in ensuring that the team stays focused on delivering a high-quality product that meets the needs of the customer.

1.  **Retrospective**: A meeting held after a sprint to reflect on the team's performance, identify areas for improvement, and develop action plans to address those areas.

1. **Scaled Agile Framework**: The **Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)** is a framework designed to help organizations implement Agile practices at scale. It provides a structured approach to align teams, improve collaboration, and deliver value more effectively across large enterprises.  
    
1. **Scrum Master**: A servant-leader role responsible for facilitating the Scrum process and ensuring that the team follows the principles and practices of Scrum. The Scrum Master is a coach, mentor, and facilitator who helps the team to self-organize, collaborate effectively, and continuously improve their processes and practices.

1. **Shippable Product**:  A version of a product that is complete, fully functional, and ready for release to customers. It meets all the necessary criteria defined in the **Definition of Done** and is capable of delivering value to users.  To align with IEC 62304, this typically includes passing quality assurance processes and delivering the comprehensive documentation set for the current iteration. 

1.  **Sprint**: A time-boxed period, typically lasting one half month (days 1-15 or 16-last day of month), during which a cross-functional Agile team collaborates to deliver a specific set of features or functionality as part of a larger project.
    
1.  **Sprint Planning**: A meeting where the team plans the work to be completed during the upcoming sprint, breaking down user stories into tasks and estimating the effort required for each task.
    
1.  **Sprint Review**: A meeting held at the end of each sprint to demonstrate the completed work to stakeholders, gather feedback, and discuss potential improvements for the next sprint.
    
1. **Sprint Retrospective**:  A meeting held after a sprint to reflect on the team's performance during the sprint, identify areas for improvement, and develop action plans to address those areas. The goal of a sprint retrospective is to facilitate continuous improvement by encouraging the team to learn from their experiences, discuss what went well and what didn't, and identify ways to improve their processes, communication, and collaboration

1. **Story Points**: See Effort.

1. **Task**: Tasks are the smallest unit of work.  The Agile Team analyzes the requirements of a User Story and then decomposes the work into Tasks.  Each task tracks the work required to deliver some functionality by an individual.

1.  **User Story**: A User Story is a brief, informal description of a feature or requirement from the perspective of the user. The User Story forms the "contract" between the product owner and the team members performing the work.
    
1.  **Velocity**: A measure of the team's productivity during a sprint, calculated by adding up the effort points of all completed user stories.
    
1.  **Work In Progress (WIP)**: The total amount of work that the team is currently working on during a sprint.
