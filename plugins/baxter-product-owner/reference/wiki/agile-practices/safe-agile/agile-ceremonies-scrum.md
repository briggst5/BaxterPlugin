# Agile Ceremonies
[[_TOC_]]


Agile ceremonies are structured meetings used in Agile methodologies to facilitate collaboration, communication, and continuous improvement within a project team. The primary ceremonies include:

1.  Sprint Planning: A meeting where the team plans the work to be completed during the upcoming sprint, breaking down user stories into tasks and estimating the effort required for each task.
    
1.  Stand-up: A brief, typically daily meeting where team members share their progress, discuss any obstacles, and coordinate their activities for the day.
    
1.  Sprint Review: A meeting held at the end of each sprint to demonstrate the completed work to stakeholders, gather feedback, and discuss potential improvements for the next sprint.
    
1.  Sprint Retrospective: A meeting held after the sprint review to reflect on the team's performance during the sprint, identify areas for improvement, and develop action plans to address those areas.
    
These ceremonies help ensure that the team stays focused, aligned, and adaptable throughout the project lifecycle.

## Sprint Planning

Sprint Planning is where the team works together to define work, including defining definition of done, setting priorities, and [estimating effort](/Agile-Practices/SAFe-Agile/SAFe-Time-Box-Planning#Work).

Attendees: _Scrum Master_, _Product Owner_, Scrum team members

When: Beginning of Sprint

Sprint planning should focus on high priority tasks (i.e., the must haves) but keep an eye on the future to ensure that the team is tending towards the desired end goal.  The following graphic shows how a large architecture can serve as a guide.  As the project moves forward in time and as clarity  the team focuses on well-defined, "shovel ready" tasks and work towards building the clarity needed for the next sprints.

Prior to the sprint planning meeting, the team will perform _Backlog Grooming_.  As the previous figure shows, some items will be clear and ready to execute, others need some effort to be ready for a sprint, and some are too broad and will need ongoing effort to get clarity.  The goal is to make sure that the project backlog is the most accurate representation of the project at that point in time.  During _Sprint Planning_, the _Scrum Master_ will facilitate the team setting the team capacity, selecting tasks to be part of the sprint, and ensuring that the sprint does not overschedule any individuals and has well-defined tasks.

## Stand-Up

A regular short meeting including the whole team, where each team member discusses progress and blockers.  Typically, we answer the questions:

* What did I do yesterday,
* What am I working on today,
* What issues are blocking me.

When Stand-Ups are working well, the whole team gains perspective on the progress that everyone is making and helps team members anticipate blocking issues.  It also serves to strengthen the team, showing that everyone is contributing to the progress, and enables others to offer assistance to remove blockers.  The daily reinforcement helps keep everyone excited about the overall progress.

Typically, we will "walk the board", reviewing the tasks.  Individuals can quickly review in progress and recently completed tasks.  It is critical that the work items carry the details - the standup is not the place to solve problems or discuss the details.

## Sprint Review

The _Sprint Review_ gathers together the Agile team and the broader stakeholders to review and demonstrate work completed during the sprint.  It provides an opportunity to showcase completed work, gather feedback, and ensure alignment with broad project goals.  

These touch points are one of the most crucial aspects of the Agile method.  The stakeholders (customer) should be able to see progress, provide feedback, and help prioritize the next steps for the project.  This helps build confidence that the project is moving in the "right direction," a concept that is surprisingly subjective.  So, having this level of buy-in is crucial.  These touch points are also critical to prevent "creeping featurism," where the customer keeps adding additional features.  This is where the _Product Owner_ has an important job to push back and keep the project focused on its planned tasks.

## Sprint Retrospective
At its core, the Agile process is recursive, continuous improvement process.  Folks often say, "I don't care what your Agile process is today, I want to know how you assess and improve it."  The _Sprint Retrospective_ is the process to improve the process.  During the retrospective, the Scrum Master facilitates a discussion with individuals to evaluate what went well and what did not; and then collate those results into a summary; and then formulate a plan to improving the way the team works. 

The Scrum Master will prompt each team member to reflect on the past sprint. There are many formal or informal ways to accomplish this including: start/stop/continue, roses/buds/thorns, mad/glad/sad, etc.  Whatever the mechanism, the goal is to establish:

* What went well during this sprint?
* What didn't go well during the sprint?
* If we could change one thing, what would it be?

The Scrum Master will then facilitate the conversation around the results.  The goal is to assess the processes that were used, and reinforce the things that worked well, and then develop a plan to change the things that did not.  The Scrum Master and Product Owner may need to engage broader leadership to resolve problems that are beyond the team's control.

This is one place where **Ego is the Enemy**.  Scrum Masters may feel some ownership in the state of the process and not want to change.  Team members can start to "pile on" to issues because they want to be seen supporting their colleagues.  This is where the whole team needs to be fully engaged in the activity, as the team controls its own destiny.
